Drug: Definition & Sources

Drug (WHO Definition): "Any substance or product that is used or intended to be used to modify or explore physiological systems or pathological states for the benefit of the recipient."

In pharmacology, a drug is defined as any chemical agent that affects living processes. It includes substances used for diagnosis, prevention, treatment, or cure of diseases. The term derives from French "drogue" meaning a dry herb.

Essential Drugs (WHO): Drugs that satisfy the priority healthcare needs of the population, selected based on disease prevalence, evidence of efficacy, safety, and cost-effectiveness. India has its own National List of Essential Medicines (NLEM).

Key Definitions

Pharmacology: The science of drugs including their origin, composition, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, therapeutic uses, and adverse effects. Derived from Greek: pharmakon (drug) + logos (study).
Agonist: A drug that binds to a receptor and produces a pharmacological response similar to the endogenous ligand. It has both affinity and intrinsic activity (e.g., Adrenaline at β-receptors).
Antagonist: A drug that binds to a receptor but does not produce a response; it blocks the action of the agonist. It has affinity but zero intrinsic activity (e.g., Propranolol at β-receptors).
Drug Tolerance: A decrease in response to a drug after repeated administration, requiring higher doses to achieve the same effect. Types: pharmacokinetic (metabolic enzyme induction) and pharmacodynamic (receptor down-regulation).
Idiosyncrasy: An abnormal, unpredictable genetic-based response to a drug, qualitatively different from the expected effect. E.g., barbiturates causing excitement instead of sedation in some individuals; primaquine causing hemolysis in G6PD deficiency.
Allergy (Drug Allergy): An immunologically mediated adverse reaction to a drug. It requires prior sensitization and is dose-independent. E.g., penicillin anaphylaxis, sulfa rash.
Loading Dose: An initial higher dose given to rapidly achieve therapeutic plasma concentration.
Loading Dose Formula
Loading Dose = Vd × Ctarget
Inverse Agonist: A drug that binds to the same receptor as an agonist but produces a pharmacological response opposite to that of the agonist. It has negative intrinsic activity. E.g., some benzodiazepine receptor ligands reduce GABA-mediated activity.

Historical Landmarks & Scope of Pharmacology

Historical Landmarks
•Ancient Era (Before 1500 AD): Use of opium (Sumerians, 4000 BC), Ebers Papyrus (Egypt, 1550 BC) listing 700+ drugs, Ayurveda (Charaka & Sushruta Samhita, 1000 BC), Chinese herbal medicine (Shennong, 2700 BC), Hippocratic oath emphasizing "do no harm."
•Materia Medica Period (1500–1800): Dioscorides wrote "De Materia Medica" (60 AD). Paracelsus (1493–1541) introduced the concept that the dose makes the poison. William Withering described digitalis use (1785).
•Modern Pharmacology (1800–1950): François Magendie (1819) — experimental pharmacology. Claude Bernard — studied curare and site of drug action. Rudolf Buchheim (1847) — established first pharmacology laboratory at Giessen. Oswald Schmiedeberg (1872) — father of modern pharmacology. Paul Ehrlich — chemotherapy concept, Salvarsan (1909). Alexander Fleming — Penicillin (1928). Gerhard Domagk — Sulfonamides (1935).
•Post-1950 Era: Discovery of oral contraceptives (1960), propranolol (1964), H2 blockers, ACE inhibitors, statins, monoclonal antibodies, and gene therapy approaches.
Scope of Pharmacology
•Pharmacokinetics: Absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion (ADME) — what the body does to the drug
•Pharmacodynamics: Mechanism of action, dose-response, receptor interactions — what the drug does to the body
•Pharmacotherapeutics: Application of drugs in treatment of disease
•Toxicology: Study of adverse effects, poisoning, and antidotes
•Clinical Pharmacology: Drug evaluation in humans — clinical trials, therapeutic drug monitoring
•Pharmacogenomics: Effect of genetic variation on drug response
•Pharmacovigilance: Ongoing safety monitoring of marketed drugs
•Pharmacoepidemiology: Study of drug use and effects in populations

Receptors: Types & G-Protein Coupled Receptors

Receptor: A specific macromolecular component of a cell (usually a protein) with which a drug interacts to produce a pharmacological response. Receptors determine the selectivity of drug action. Receptor occupation theory: Response ∝ fraction of receptors occupied.
Classification of Receptors
TypeMechanismExamples
1. Ligand-Gated Ion Channels (Ionotropic)Ligand binding opens ion channel directly; fast response (ms)nAChR (Na⁺/K⁺), GABAA (Cl⁻), NMDA (Ca²⁺), 5-HT3
2. G-Protein Coupled Receptors (GPCR)Ligand binding → G-protein activation → second messenger; moderate speed (sec)Muscarinic, β-adrenergic, α2-adrenergic, 5-HT1,2,4, opioid
3. Enzyme-Linked ReceptorsIntracellular enzymatic domain (kinase) activated; slower (min)Insulin receptor (tyrosine kinase), GF receptors
4. Intracellular / Nuclear ReceptorsLigand diffuses in; alters gene transcription; slowest (hours)Steroid receptors (GR, MR, AR, ER), thyroid receptor, PPAR
5. Transporters / Carrier ProteinsNot classical receptors but can be drug targetsNET, DAT, SERT (target of antidepressants)
G-Protein Coupled Receptors (GPCRs) — Detailed

GPCRs are the largest family of membrane receptors (~800 in humans). They share a common 7-transmembrane α-helical structure. They transduce extracellular signals via heterotrimeric G-proteins.

s → AC ↑ → cAMP ↑ Gi → AC ↓ → cAMP ↓ Gq → PLC → IP₃/DAG 7-Transmembrane Receptor
Figure: GPCR Signal Transduction Mechanism
G-Protein Signaling Steps
1.Resting State: GPCR is unbound; Gα has GDP bound; Gαβγ trimer is associated with receptor.
2.Ligand Binding: Agonist binds to extracellular domain → conformational change in receptor.
3.G-Protein Activation: Receptor acts as GEF (guanine nucleotide exchange factor) → GDP replaced by GTP on Gα → Gα-GTP dissociates from Gβγ.
4.Effector Activation: Gα-GTP and/or Gβγ subunits regulate effector enzymes/ion channels.
5.Second Messenger Generation: Effectors produce second messengers (cAMP, IP₃, DAG, cGMP, Ca²⁺).
6.Cellular Response: Second messengers activate kinases (PKA, PKC) → phosphorylation of target proteins → physiological response.
7.Termination: Gα has intrinsic GTPase activity → hydrolyzes GTP to GDP → Gα-GDP reassociates with Gβγ → returns to resting state.

Drug-Receptor Interactions & Signal Transduction
•Agonist: Binds → activates receptor → full response (e.g., Isoprenaline at β₂)
•Partial Agonist: Binds → submaximal response even at full receptor occupancy (e.g., Pindolol)
•Competitive Antagonist: Binds to same site as agonist; surmountable with high agonist dose; shifts DRC rightward (e.g., Propranolol)
•Non-competitive Antagonist: Binds to allosteric site or irreversibly; reduces maximal response; insurmountable (e.g., Phenoxybenzamine)
•Inverse Agonist: Binds → produces opposite effect of agonist; stabilizes inactive conformation (e.g., some β-blockers)

Therapeutic Index, Combined Drug Effects & ADR Overview

Therapeutic Index (TI)
Therapeutic Index: A quantitative measure of the relative safety of a drug. It is the ratio of the dose producing toxic effect to the dose producing desired therapeutic effect.
Therapeutic Index
TI = TD50 / ED50 = LD50 / ED50

TD₅₀ = dose producing toxicity in 50% of subjects; ED₅₀ = dose producing desired effect in 50%; LD₅₀ = lethal dose in 50%

Higher TI = safer drug. Example: Penicillin has very high TI; Digoxin has low TI (narrow therapeutic window).

Therapeutic Window: The range between minimum effective concentration (MEC) and minimum toxic concentration (MTC). More clinically useful than TI. For digoxin: 0.8–2.0 ng/mL.
Certain Safety Factor (CSF)
CSF = TD1 / ED99
Combined Drug Effects
TypeDefinitionExample
AdditiveCombined effect = sum of individual effects (1+1=2)Aspirin + Paracetamol (analgesia)
SynergismCombined effect > sum of individual effects (1+1>2)Sulfamethoxazole + Trimethoprim (cotrimoxazole)
PotentiationOne drug has no effect alone but enhances the other (0+1>1)Levodopa + Carbidopa
AntagonismCombined effect < sum of individual effects (1+1<2)Acetylcholine + Atropine
•Competitive Antagonism: Antagonist competes with agonist for same receptor site; parallel rightward shift of DRC; same Emax achievable with higher dose. E.g., Propranolol vs Isoprenaline.
•Non-competitive Antagonism: Antagonist binds to different site or irreversibly; flattening of DRC; Emax reduced; not surmountable. E.g., Phenoxybenzamine vs Noradrenaline.

Merits of Oral Route
•Safest, most convenient, and economical route
•Self-administration possible — no need for trained personnel
•Painless — no sterile precautions needed
•Various dosage forms available (tablet, capsule, syrup, suspension)
•Suitable for prolonged therapy
•Blank tablet form allows placebo use in clinical trials
Demerits of Oral Route
•First-pass metabolism: Drug passes through liver before systemic circulation → significant loss (e.g., propranolol ~70%, morphine ~60%, nitroglycerin ~90%)
•Slow onset: Absorption takes 30–60 min; not suitable for emergencies
•Irritant drugs can cause gastric ulcers, nausea, vomiting (e.g., NSAIDs)
•Variable absorption affected by food, pH, gastric emptying, GI motility
•Not possible in unconscious, vomiting, or uncooperative patients
•Some drugs destroyed by gastric acid or enzymes (e.g., insulin, penicillin G)
Advantages of Intravenous Route
•100% bioavailability — bypasses all barriers; no first-pass effect
•Immediate onset — within seconds; ideal for emergencies (e.g., adrenaline in anaphylaxis)
•Precise control over plasma drug levels
•Large volumes can be infused (fluid resuscitation)
•Irritant drugs can be given (diluted in large blood volume)
•Usable in unconscious patients
Disadvantages of Intravenous Route
•Irreversible — once injected, cannot be withdrawn
•Requires trained personnel and sterile equipment
•Risk of infection, thrombophlebitis, embolism
•Rapid bolus can cause severe toxicity (e.g., IV digoxin → cardiac arrest)
•Painful; not suitable for self-administration
Merits of Parenteral Route (General)
•Bypasses GI tract — no first-pass metabolism, no gastric degradation
•Rapid onset (IV > IM > SC)
•Accurate dose — no variability from absorption
•Useful in unconscious, vomiting, or malabsorption patients
Demerits of Parenteral Route
•Requires trained personnel; painful; risk of infection, abscess, nerve injury
•Cannot be easily withdrawn once administered
•More expensive; requires sterile equipment
•Psychological fear of injections (trypanophobia)

Factors Affecting Drug Absorption

Drug Absorption: The process by which a drug moves from the site of administration into the systemic circulation. Most drugs are absorbed by passive diffusion across lipid membranes.
A. Drug-Related Factors
1.Lipid Solubility: More lipid-soluble (higher lipid/water partition coefficient) drugs diffuse more readily across cell membranes. E.g., thiopentone (highly lipid soluble) crosses BBB rapidly; penicillin G (lipid insoluble) does not.
2.Degree of Ionization (pH-partition hypothesis): Unionized form is lipid-soluble and absorbed; ionized form is water-soluble and not absorbed.
Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation
For weak acid: pKa = pH + log([HA]/[A⁻])  |  For weak base: pKa = pH + log([B]/[BH⁺])
Weak acids (pKa 3–5) are primarily unionized in acidic stomach → well absorbed from stomach. Weak bases (pKa 8–10) are unionized in alkaline intestine → well absorbed from intestine.
3.Molecular Size: Small molecules (<500 Da) diffuse more easily. Large molecules require carrier-mediated or active transport.
4.Dosage Form: Solutions > suspensions > capsules > compressed tablets (in order of absorption speed). Disintegration and dissolution rate are rate-limiting for tablets.
5.Chemical Nature: Drugs structurally similar to nutrients may use carrier transport (e.g., L-DOPA uses amino acid transporter).
B. Patient-Related Factors
6.Gastric Emptying Rate: Faster emptying → drug reaches intestine sooner (larger surface area) → faster absorption. Delayed by food, anticholinergics, opioids. Accelerated by metoclopramin.
7.GI Motility: Increased motility reduces absorption time; decreased motility increases contact time but may not help if drug is poorly permeable.
8.Gastric pH: Affects ionization and stability of drugs. Antacids ↑ pH → affect absorption of ketoconazole (needs acid), etc.
9.Food: Can increase (griseofulvin with fatty meal), decrease (tetracycline with milk — chelation), or delay absorption.
10.Surface Area: Intestine has villi + microvilli (~200 m²) >> stomach surface area → most absorption occurs in small intestine regardless of drug properties.
11.GI Blood Flow: Increased perfusion enhances absorption (post-prandial state).
12.Presence of Other Agents: Adsorbents (charcoal), chelating agents, enzyme inhibitors can affect absorption.
C. Factors Affecting Parenteral Absorption
•Blood flow to injection site: Exercised muscle has higher blood flow → faster IM absorption
•Drug formulation: Aqueous solutions absorb faster than oily suspensions or depot preparations
•Molecular size: Larger molecules absorb slower from SC/IM sites
•pKa & ionization: Unionized drugs diffuse faster from injection site into blood
•Vasoactive substances: Vasodilators increase; vasoconstrictors (epinephrine with LA) decrease absorption
•Volume & concentration: Larger volumes may create pressure and spread, increasing absorption area
•Tissue permeability: SC tissue is less vascular than muscle → SC < IM < IV in absorption speed

Plasma Protein Binding & Blood-Brain Barrier

1. Plasma Protein Binding
Plasma Protein Binding: Many drugs reversibly bind to plasma proteins (mainly albumin for acidic drugs, α₁-acid glycoprotein for basic drugs). Only the unbound (free) fraction is pharmacologically active and available for distribution, metabolism, and excretion.
•Significance: Bound drug acts as a reservoir → prolongs duration of action; reduces free drug concentration; limits distribution (large molecules can't cross membranes when bound).
•Highly bound drugs (>90%): Warfarin (99%), Phenytoin (90%), Diazepam (98%), NSAIDs. Small displacement can cause large increase in free fraction → toxicity.
•Clinical importance of displacement interactions: Warfarin displaced by phenylbutazone/sulfonamides → increased bleeding. Phenytoin displaced by valproic acid → increased toxicity.
•Does NOT usually affect steady-state level (free concentration returns to original as total decreases due to increased elimination of displaced drug), but can cause transient toxicity.
Volume of Distribution (Vd)
Vd = Amount of drug in body / Plasma drug concentration
2. Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)
BBB: A specialized barrier formed by brain capillary endothelial cells with tight junctions, astrocyte foot processes, and a basement membrane. It restricts passage of most drugs from blood into brain tissue.
•Features: Tight junctions between endothelial cells (no fenestrations), limited pinocytosis, presence of P-glycoprotein efflux pump, astrocyte coverage.
•Drugs that CROSS BBB: Highly lipid-soluble (thiopentone, diazepam), small molecular weight, unionized at blood pH, non-protein bound. Also use specific transporters (L-DOPA uses L-amino acid transporter).
•Drugs that DO NOT cross BBB: Highly ionized (heparin, penicillin G), large molecules (insulin), highly protein-bound drugs, substrates of P-gp efflux.
•BBB is disrupted in: Meningitis, encephalitis, brain tumors, trauma → drugs that normally don't cross may enter → useful for antibiotic treatment of meningitis.
Volume of Distribution interpretation: High Vd (>0.6 L/kg) = drug concentrated in tissues (e.g., digoxin 7 L/kg). Low Vd (<0.3 L/kg) = drug mainly in plasma (e.g., warfarin 0.1 L/kg).

Drug Metabolism (Biotransformation) & Role of Enzymes

Drug Metabolism: The enzymatic conversion of drugs into metabolites, generally rendering them more water-soluble (polar) for easier excretion. Site: primarily liver (hepatocytes); also kidney, lung, intestine, plasma, skin.
Phase I Reactions (Functionalization / Modification)

Introduce or expose a functional group (–OH, –COOH, –NH₂) on the drug molecule. Usually inactivate the drug, but can also activate prodrugs or generate toxic metabolites.

Reaction TypeEnzyme SystemExample
OxidationCYP450 (microsomal); MAO, flavin MO (mitochondrial)Phenytoin → HPPA; Barbiturates; Ethanol → Acetaldehyde (ALDH)

DecyclizationCYP450Barbiturate ring opening
CYP450 System: Cytochrome P450 is a superfamily of heme-containing enzymes located in smooth endoplasmic reticulum. Major families: CYP3A4 (metabolizes ~50% drugs), CYP2D6, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP1A2. Requires NADPH and O₂. Located primarily in liver.
Phase II Reactions (Conjugation / Synthetic)

Attach a polar moiety to the drug or Phase I metabolite. Always inactivates the drug (except few exceptions like morphine-6-glucuronide). Mostly non-microsomal (cytosolic) enzymes.

Enzyme Induction
Enzyme Induction: Increased synthesis of metabolizing enzymes (especially CYP450) by certain drugs, leading to decreased drug effect (faster metabolism).
•Inducers: Phenobarbitone, Rifampicin, Carbamazepine, Phenytoin, Griseofulvin, St. John's Wort, Alcohol (chronic)
•Clinical consequence: Warfarin + Rifampicin → decreased INR → clot risk. Oral contraceptives + Phenytoin → breakthrough bleeding/failure. Auto-induction (carbamazepine accelerates its own metabolism).
Enzyme Inhibition
Enzyme Inhibition: Decreased activity of metabolizing enzymes, leading to increased drug effect (slower metabolism). Usually competitive and reversible.
•Inhibitors: Cimetidine, Erythromycin, Ketoconazole, Grapefruit juice, Metronidazole, Isoniazid, Allopurinol
•Clinical consequence: Warfarin + Cimetidine → increased INR → bleeding. Statins + Erythromycin → rhabdomyolysis. Terfenadine + Ketoconazole → fatal arrhythmia (QT prolongation).

Zero Order & First Order Kinetics of Drug Elimination

Zero Order Kinetics
Zero Order Elimination: A constant amount of drug is eliminated per unit time, regardless of drug concentration. Elimination rate is independent of concentration.
Zero Order Rate Equation
dC/dt = –K0   →   Ct = C0 – K0 × t
•Plasma concentration vs time plot is a straight line (linear decline)
•Half-life is NOT constant — increases with increasing concentration
•Occurs when metabolizing enzymes are saturated (capacity-limited / Michaelis-Menten kinetics)
•Examples: Ethanol (at moderate-high doses), Phenytoin (at therapeutic doses), Aspirin (at high doses), Theophylline
Clinical implication: Small increase in dose can cause disproportionate increase in plasma concentration → toxicity. Phenytoin dose adjustment requires careful monitoring.
First Order Kinetics
First Order Elimination: A constant fraction/percentage of drug is eliminated per unit time. Elimination rate is proportional to drug concentration.
First Order Rate Equation
dC/dt = –Ke × Ct   →   Ct = C0 × e–Ket   →   log Ct = log C0 – (Ke/2.303) × t
Half-Life
t½ = 0.693 / Ke
•Log concentration vs time plot is a straight line (exponential decline on linear scale)
•Half-life is CONSTANT — independent of concentration
•Applies to majority of drugs at therapeutic concentrations (enzymes NOT saturated)
•Time to reach steady-state = 4–5 half-lives regardless of dose
Clearance
Cl = Ke × Vd   |   Cl = Rate of elimination / Cp

Drug Interactions — Classification & Pharmacokinetic Interactions

Drug Interaction: When the effects of one drug are altered by the concurrent administration of another drug, food, or chemical agent. Can be beneficial or harmful.
Classification
A.Pharmacokinetic Interactions: One drug alters the ADME of another — changes plasma concentration without altering pharmacological effect per se.
B.Pharmacodynamic Interactions: Both drugs reach the site of action but their effects are modified at the receptor/physiological level.

•Additive: Alcohol + Diazepam → excessive CNS depression
•Synergism: Sulfamethoxazole + Trimethoprim → sequential blockade of folate synthesis
•Potentiation: Allopurinol + 6-MP → enhanced antineoplastic effect

Adverse Drug Reactions — Rawlins-Thompson Classification

ADR (WHO): "A response to a drug which is noxious and unintended, and which occurs at doses normally used in man for the prophylaxis, diagnosis, or therapy of disease, or for the modification of physiological function."
Rawlins-Thompson Classification (1977)
Type A — Augmented (Predictable, Dose-dependent)

Based on pharmacological properties of the drug; dose-dependent; predictable from known pharmacology; account for ~80% of ADRs.

Examples: Hypotension from antihypertensives; Bleeding from warfarin; Bradycardia from propranolol; Hypoglycemia from insulin; Atropine-like effects of TCAs (dry mouth, constipation, blurred vision); GI irritation from NSAIDs.

Type B — Bizarre (Unpredictable, Non-dose-dependent)

Not related to pharmacological action; idiosyncratic or immunological; not predictable; independent of dose; account for ~10–15% but are more serious.

Examples: Drug allergy (penicillin anaphylaxis, sulfa rash); Idiosyncrasy (barbiturate excitement, primaquine hemolysis in G6PD deficiency); Agranulocytosis from chloramphenicol; SJS/TEN from carbamazepine.

Type C — Chronic (Continuous, Dose & Time-related)

Occurs with prolonged use; related to cumulative dose.

Examples: Osteoporosis from long-term corticosteroids; Tardive dyskinesia from long-term antipsychotics; Analgesic nephropathy from chronic NSAID use.

Type D — Delayed (Time-related)

Appears after some time, even after drug withdrawal.

Examples: Teratogenicity (thalidomide phocomelia); Carcinogenicity (alkylating agents); Cyanocobalamin deficiency from metformin (years).

Type E — End of Use (Withdrawal)

Occurs after cessation of drug, usually due to adaptive physiological changes.

Examples: Opioid withdrawal (agitation, lacrimation, diarrhea); Benzodiazepine withdrawal (anxiety, insomnia, seizures); Corticosteroid withdrawal (adrenal crisis); β-blocker withdrawal (rebound tachycardia, angina).

Type F — Failure (Therapeutic Failure)

Unexpected failure of therapy.

Examples: Antibiotic resistance; Oral contraceptive failure with enzyme inducers; Loss of efficacy in Parkinson's with long-term levodopa.

Tolerance, Dependence & Addiction

Drug Tolerance: Decreased response to a drug after repeated administration, requiring progressively larger doses to achieve the same effect.
•Pharmacokinetic tolerance: Increased metabolism (enzyme induction) → lower plasma levels. E.g., Barbiturates induce their own metabolism; Tolerance to ethanol.
•Pharmacodynamic tolerance: Cellular adaptive changes (receptor down-regulation, desensitization) → reduced response. E.g., Tolerance to morphine analgesia; Tachyphylaxis to ephedrine.
•Cross-tolerance: Tolerance to one drug confers tolerance to related drugs. E.g., Alcohol ↔ benzodiazepines; Morphine ↔ methadone.
•Tachyphylaxis: Rapid development of tolerance on repeated dosing within short time intervals. E.g., Ephedrine, amphetamine (due to depletion of neuronal catecholamine stores).
Drug Dependence: A state in which the individual relies on a drug for normal functioning. Physical and/or psychological components.
•Physical dependence: Adaptive physiological changes → withdrawal syndrome on cessation. E.g., Opioids, alcohol, benzodiazepines, corticosteroids. Withdrawal is typically opposite of drug effect.
•Psychological dependence: Compulsive drug-seeking behavior for emotional/psychological comfort; no physical withdrawal but intense craving. E.g., Cocaine, nicotine, cannabis.
Drug Addiction: A chronic, relapsing brain disorder characterized by compulsive drug seeking and use despite harmful consequences. It involves the reward pathway (mesolimbic dopamine system). Key features: loss of control, craving, continued use despite harm.
•Factors in development: Genetic predisposition, pharmacological properties of drug (rapid onset, short duration = higher addiction potential), environmental factors (peer pressure, availability), psychological factors (stress, trauma, mental illness).
•High addiction potential drugs: Heroin, cocaine, nicotine, alcohol, methamphetamine. The faster the drug reaches brain (IV > smoking > oral), the higher the addiction potential.
•Drug Abuse: Use of a drug for non-therapeutic purposes or in a manner inconsistent with medical/social norms. Not synonymous with addiction (recreational use ≠ addiction).

Neurohumoral Transmission & Adrenergic Neurotransmitter Synthesis

Neurohumoral Transmission: The process by which nerve impulses are transmitted across synapses and neuroeffector junctions through the release of chemical neurotransmitters that act on specific receptors on the postsynaptic membrane.
Steps of Neurohumoral Transmission
1.Impulse conduction: Action potential arrives at nerve terminal → depolarization of presynaptic membrane.
2.Ca²⁺ influx: Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ enters terminal.
3.Vesicle fusion & release: Ca²⁺ triggers fusion of synaptic vesicles with presynaptic membrane → exocytosis of neurotransmitter into synaptic cleft.
4.Receptor interaction: Neurotransmitter diffuses across cleft → binds to postsynaptic receptors → conformational change → biological response.
5.Termination: Neurotransmitter action is terminated by: (a) Reuptake (most common — e.g., NET, DAT, SERT); (b) Enzymatic degradation (AChE for ACh, MAO/COMT for catecholamines); (c) Diffusion away from cleft.

Pharmacological Uses of Acetylcholine

Acetylcholine (ACh): The primary neurotransmitter at cholinergic synapses (neuromuscular junction, autonomic ganglia, parasympathetic neuroeffector junctions, some CNS synapses). Synthesized from choline + acetyl-CoA by choline acetyltransferase (ChAT). Degraded by acetylcholinesterase (AChE).
Important: ACh itself has no therapeutic use due to: (a) Rapid hydrolysis by AChE and plasma pseudocholinesterase; (b) Non-selective action on all cholinergic receptors (muscarinic + nicotinic); (c) Multiple undesirable effects. Instead, cholinergic agonists and anticholinesterases are used clinically.
Pharmacological Actions of ACh (basis for understanding cholinergic drugs)
•Cardiovascular: ↓ Heart rate (M₂), ↓ AV conduction, ↓ contractility, vasodilation (M₃ on endothelium → NO release) → ↓ BP
•GI tract: ↑ Motility, ↑ secretion (M₃), relaxes sphincters → may cause cramps, diarrhea
•Respiratory: Bronchoconstriction (M₃), ↑ bronchial secretion
•Eye: Miosis (M₃), accommodation for near vision, ↓ IOP
•Bladder: Detrusor contraction (M₃), sphincter relaxation → micturition
•Glands: ↑ Sweating, salivation, lacrimation
•NMJ: Muscle contraction (Nₙ — nicotinic), then paralysis (depolarizing block at high doses)
•Ganglia: Stimulation (Nₙ) → sympathetic + parasympathetic effects

Organophosphorus Poisoning & Anticholinesterase Uses

Organophosphorus (OP) Poisoning
Organophosphates: Irreversible anticholinesterases that phosphorylate the serine hydroxyl group at the active site of AChE. The phosphorylated enzyme is extremely stable (aging — loss of an alkyl group makes it irreversibly inactivated). Common agents: Parathion, Malathion (agricultural), Sarin, Soman (nerve gases), Dichlorvos.
Signs & Symptoms (SLUDGE + DUMBELS)
•SLUDGE: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis
•DUMBELS: Diarrhea, Urination, Miosis, Bronchospasm, Excitation (CNS), Lacrimation, Salivation
•Muscle: Fasciculations → flaccid paralysis (NMJ overstimulation → depolarizing block)
•CNS: Anxiety, restlessness, confusion, convulsions → coma, respiratory depression
•Cardiovascular: Bradycardia, hypotension (early); tachycardia, hypertension (CNS-mediated, late)
Treatment of OP Poisoning
1.Atropine (Antidote for muscarinic effects): Large doses (2–5 mg IV, repeated). Blocks muscarinic receptors → controls secretions, bronchospasm, bradycardia, GI effects. Does NOT reverse NMJ paralysis or CNS effects.
2.Pralidoxime (2-PAM) (Antidote for nicotinic effects): Oxime that reactivates phosphorylated AChE by cleaving the P-O bond. Must be given early (<24 hrs, before "aging"). Given IV 1–2 g. Reverses NMJ paralysis and muscle fasciculations. Does NOT cross BBB well.
3.Supportive care: Airway maintenance, artificial ventilation, gastric lavage (if oral ingestion), activated charcoal, diazepam for seizures.
4.Decontamination: Remove contaminated clothing, wash skin thoroughly (important for dermal exposure).
Therapeutic Uses of Anticholinesterases
•Myasthenia Gravis: Neostigmine, Pyridostigmine → improve muscle strength by increasing ACh at NMJ
•Post-operative paralytic ileus / urinary retention: Neostigmine → ↑ GI motility and bladder tone
•Alzheimer's disease: Donepezil, Rivastigmine, Galantamine → enhance cholinergic transmission in brain
•Glaucoma (narrow angle): Physostigmine, Echothiophate (long-acting) → miosis → open trabecular meshwork
•Antidote for curare/competitive NMJ block: Neostigmine reverses non-depolarizing block
